SafeSteps

Applied NLP · safety · real-world data

Overview

SafeSteps was built to handle messy, user-generated hazard data and turn it into something actionable. The goal was to move beyond a simple text classifier and build a system that could realistically support safer routing and decision-making.

It combines NLP-based classification with geospatial context, sitting somewhere between a lightweight ML system and a practical real-world application.

What made it difficult

The data was highly unstructured. Reports were short, inconsistent, full of typos, and heavily imbalanced. The most critical hazards were often the rarest, making them the hardest to model effectively.

Another challenge was that categories were not fixed. As more data was explored, edge cases kept appearing that didn’t cleanly fit into predefined labels, requiring ongoing adjustments to the labeling strategy.

Approach

A flexible pipeline was built using MongoDB for storage and lightweight NLP models for classification, allowing for fast iteration and easy inspection of results.

Beyond classification, the system incorporated basic geospatial reasoning to prioritize hazards based on severity, frequency, and historical patterns. The focus was not on complexity, but on building something interpretable and adaptable.

Iteration and debugging

Progress came primarily from analyzing failure cases. Misclassifications were grouped and examined to determine whether the issue came from the data, the labeling, or the model itself.

Some augmentation strategies failed because they didn’t reflect how users actually write. This required adjusting both preprocessing and labeling approaches to better match real-world inputs.

Evaluation

Evaluation focused on per-class performance rather than overall accuracy. Recall for rare but critical hazards was prioritized, since missing these cases would significantly reduce the system’s usefulness.

False positives were also manually reviewed, since excessive noise in predictions could reduce trust in the system.

Takeaways

This project reinforced that in real-world NLP systems, data quality and labeling strategy often matter more than model choice.

It also highlighted the importance of designing systems that can handle messy, evolving inputs rather than relying on fixed assumptions about data structure.
